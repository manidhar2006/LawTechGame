Rule 1: Short Title and Commencement
Rule 1 serves as the foundational temporal marker for the entire regulatory framework, establishing a staggered rollout that prioritizes the establishment of regulatory infrastructure over substantive compliance burdens.1 The notification date of November 13, 2025, triggers an immediate implementation of administrative provisions, specifically Rules 1, 2, and 17 to 21, which govern definitions and the constitution of the Data Protection Board.1 This ensures that the adjudicatory and oversight mechanisms are in place before organizations are held accountable for substantive obligations.6
The secondary phase occurs twelve months post-publication (November 2026), activating Rule 4 concerning the registration and obligations of Consent Managers, who represent a new class of intermediaries in the data ecosystem.1 The final phase, occurring eighteen months post-publication (May 2027), activates the core compliance obligations including notice, security safeguards, breach notifications, and data principal rights.5 This strategic delay acknowledges the profound technical and organizational shifts required for Data Fiduciaries to achieve full compliance.6Rule 1: Assessment Module

Rule 2: Definitions
Rule 2 provides the semantic bridge between the statutory definitions of the Act and the operational procedures of the Rules. It adopts a holistic approach by incorporating all definitions from the DPDP Act 2023, while introducing specialized terms like "techno-legal measures" and "user account".1 The definition of "user account" is deliberately broad, encompassing profiles, handles, email addresses, and mobile numbers, which prevents Data Fiduciaries from segmenting user data into silos to avoid notice or rights obligations.1
The term "techno-legal measures" is vital for the Board's operation as a "digital office," referring to the specific technical tools and legal procedures that allow for remote evidence, digital service of summons, and online adjudication.1 This rule ensures that the Board and the Appellate Tribunal (TDSAT) can function without requiring physical presence, aligning with the "Digital India" vision of streamlined, tech-enabled justice.1

Rule 3: Notice Given by Data Fiduciary to Data Principal
Rule 3 mandates a radical shift in transparency by requiring "standalone" notices that are independent of general terms and conditions.1 This provision addresses the "illusory consent" problem, where privacy terms are buried in dozens of pages of legalese. A Rule 3 notice must be clear, itemized, and available in multiple languages to ensure inclusivity across India's diverse demographic landscape.1
The notice must granularly describe the categories of personal data being processed and the specific purpose for each category.1 Furthermore, it must provide immediate pathways (links or other means) for the Data Principal to withdraw consent with the same ease as it was given, exercise statutory rights, or lodge a complaint with the Data Protection Board.1 This rule establishes the "notice-and-consent" architecture as an auditable transaction rather than a one-time passive acceptance.3

Rule 4: Registration and Obligations of Consent Manager
Rule 4 introduces the "Consent Manager" (CM), a specialized intermediary that acts as a privacy advocate for the Data Principal.1 Consent Managers are intended to resolve the problem of "consent fragmentation" by providing a single interoperable dashboard where individuals can manage permissions across multiple Data Fiduciaries.6 To ensure institutional trust, Rule 4 and the First Schedule impose high entry barriers, including a minimum net worth of 2 Crore Rupees and a requirement to be an Indian-incorporated company.1
A Consent Manager operates in a fiduciary capacity toward the Data Principal, meaning they must prioritize the individual’s interests and avoid conflicts with Data Fiduciaries.1 Technically, CMs must be "blind" to the content of the data they facilitate; they manage the permissions but must not be able to read the data moving between fiduciaries.1 They are also required to maintain logs of all consent-related transactions for at least seven years, providing a robust audit trail for any privacy disputes.1

Rule 5: Processing for Provision of Subsidies, Benefits, and Services
Rule 5 addresses the State’s authority to process personal data for governance and welfare purposes. It provides a formal standard for "legitimate uses" under Section 7(b), ensuring that the government can use personal data to deliver subsidies, services, and licenses funded by the public exchequer.1 This is particularly relevant for the "India Stack" and Aadhaar-enabled services where data flows between multiple departments.2
However, this authority is not absolute. Rule 5 and the Second Schedule mandate that government processing must adhere to principles of lawfulness, purpose limitation, and data minimization.1 Even when utilizing data from existing digitised registers, the State must implement "appropriate technical and organisational measures" to ensure accuracy and consistency.1 By requiring accountability for the individuals who determine the means of processing, Rule 5 attempts to replicate private-sector data governance standards within the public sector.1

Rule 6: Reasonable Security Safeguards
Rule 6 is the most critical technical provision of the framework, defining the "reasonable security safeguards" required under Section 8(5) of the Act.1 Failure to implement these safeguards is penalized most severely, with potential fines of up to 250 Crore Rupees.1 The rule moves away from a purely subjective standard, mandating specific technical measures such as end-to-end encryption, obfuscation, masking, and the use of virtual tokens.1
Operational requirements under Rule 6 include a mandatory "1-year log retention floor" for access logs and traffic data to facilitate forensic discovery.1 Fiduciaries are also held vicariously liable for the security of their Data Processors; they must contractually obligate vendors to maintain equivalent security standards.28 This prevents "liability offshoring" through the use of third-party cloud or analytics providers.26

Rule 7: Intimation of Personal Data Breach
Rule 7 establishes a mandatory and aggressive timeline for reporting data breaches. Fiduciaries must notify the affected Data Principals and the Data Protection Board "without delay" upon becoming aware of a breach.1 For the Board, this initial intimation must be followed by a detailed and comprehensive report within 72 hours, covering the nature, extent, location, and potential impact of the incident.1
The notice to the Data Principal is designed to be actionable, requiring fiduciaries to provide a clear description of the breach and specific "safety measures" the user can take to protect their own interests (e.g., changing passwords).1 This rule emphasizes that transparency is a critical component of risk mitigation. Failure to notify the Board or affected principals can attract a penalty of up to 200 Crore Rupees, significantly higher than the cost of implementing a robust incident response plan.12

Rule 8: Time Period for Specified Purpose (Retention/Erasure)
Rule 8 implements the principle of "storage limitation," mandating that fiduciaries erase personal data once the "specified purpose" is no longer being served.1 To provide objective clarity, the Third Schedule defines "exhaustion of purpose" timelines for major digital platforms based on user activity. For instance, e-commerce and social media platforms with over 2 crore users must erase data if there is no contact/rights exercise for three years.1
A proactive transparency measure in Rule 8(2) requires fiduciaries to give users a "48-hour warning" before their data is deleted due to inactivity, allowing them to log in and reset the timer.1 However, even after erasure of account data, Rule 8(3) mandates a "universal 1-year retention floor" for associated traffic data and processing logs to support regulatory inquiries.1 This ensures a balance between the right to be forgotten and the state’s interest in investigating cyber incidents.10

Rule 16: Exemption for Research, Archiving, or Statistical Purposes
Rule 16 provides a specific exemption for data processing essential for academic research, public archiving, or statistical analysis.1 To ensure this is not used for unauthorized commercial profiling, the rule requires adherence to the standards of the Second Schedule—the same accountability standards applied to government welfare processing.1
