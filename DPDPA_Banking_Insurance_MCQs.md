# PRINCIPLE 1 — LAWFUL PROCESSING

---

**Q1. A private bank collects and processes the KYC data of a customer to comply with RBI's Know-Your-Customer guidelines. Under DPDPA, this processing is:**

- A) Unlawful as the customer has not given explicit consent under DPDPA
- B) Lawful under Section 7(d) as it fulfills an obligation under law to disclose information to the State
- C) Unlawful unless the bank is a Significant Data Fiduciary
- D) Lawful only if the customer signs a DPDPA-specific consent form

✅ **Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Section 7(d) allows processing for fulfilling any obligation under any law in force in India to disclose information to the State or its instrumentalities. KYC compliance under RBI regulations is a statutory obligation, making such processing lawful without requiring separate DPDPA consent.

---

**Q2. An insurance company processes a policyholder's medical records to assess her claim. The company later uses the same data to market new health plans without seeking fresh consent. The marketing use is:**

- A) Lawful as the data was already collected
- B) Lawful if disclosed in the policy document
- C) Unlawful as marketing is not a legitimate use under Section 7 and requires fresh consent
- D) Lawful as the insurance company is a licensed entity

✅ **Correct Answer: C**

**Explanation:** Under Section 7, marketing is not listed as a legitimate use for processing without consent. The claim assessment was lawful, but using the same data for marketing constitutes a new purpose requiring fresh and explicit consent from the policyholder under Section 6.

---

**Q3. A bank processes customer data to comply with a court order for disclosure in a loan default recovery case. This is lawful under which provision?**

- A) Section 7(a) — voluntary provision of data
- B) Section 7(e) — compliance with a judgment or decree
- C) Section 7(b) — State provision of services
- D) Section 6 — consent of Data Principal

✅ **Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Section 7(e) specifically allows processing for compliance with any judgment or decree or order issued under any law in force in India. A court order for disclosure in a loan default recovery case directly falls under this provision.

---

**Q4. A fintech company processes customer financial data claiming it is for "AI model training to improve services." Under DPDPA, this processing is:**

- A) Lawful as it improves customer experience
- B) Lawful if the AI model is developed in India
- C) Unlawful as AI model training is not a legitimate use under Section 7 and requires explicit consent
- D) Lawful if disclosed in the terms and conditions

✅ **Correct Answer: C**

**Explanation:** AI model training is not listed as a legitimate use under Section 7. Without explicit consent from the customer, using their financial data for AI training is unlawful under Section 4. The fintech company must obtain specific informed consent for this purpose.

---

**Q5. An insurer processes data of a policyholder to ascertain her financial information after she defaults on premium payments. This falls under:**

- A) Section 7(b) — State benefit provision
- B) Section 7(f) — ascertaining financial information of a defaulter
- C) Section 6 — explicit consent
- D) Section 9 — children's data processing

✅ **Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Section 17(1)(f) allows processing for ascertaining financial information and assets and liabilities of any person who has defaulted in payment due on account of a loan or advance taken from a financial institution. Insurance premium default falls within this category.

---

**Q6. A bank shares customer data with the Financial Intelligence Unit (FIU) under the Prevention of Money Laundering Act (PMLA). Under DPDPA, this sharing is:**

- A) Unlawful as customer consent is not obtained
- B) Lawful under Section 7(d) as it fulfills a legal obligation to disclose information to the State
- C) Unlawful unless approved by the Data Protection Board
- D) Lawful only for amounts above ₹10 lakhs

✅ **Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** PMLA mandates reporting suspicious transactions to FIU-India. This is a statutory obligation. Section 7(d) covers processing for fulfilling any obligation under any law in force in India to disclose information to the State or its instrumentalities, making such sharing lawful without customer consent.

---

**Q7. An NBFC collects customer data through its mobile app for loan processing. It also quietly enrolls customers in a third-party loyalty rewards program without informing them. The enrollment is:**

- A) Lawful as loyalty programs benefit the customer
- B) Lawful if mentioned in the app's general terms
- C) Unlawful as neither consent nor a legitimate use under Section 7 covers undisclosed third-party enrollment
- D) Lawful if the NBFC and the loyalty program are affiliated companies

✅ **Correct Answer: C**

**Explanation:** Section 4 requires all processing to be either consented to or covered by a legitimate use. Silently enrolling customers in a third-party program without their knowledge satisfies neither requirement. There is no informed consent and no applicable Section 7 legitimate use for this purpose.

---

**Q8. A life insurance company processes customer health data to assess mortality risk for actuarial calculations. This processing is:**

- A) Unlawful as health data is sensitive
- B) Lawful as it is for the specified purpose of insurance underwriting with customer consent
- C) Lawful only if the company is a Significant Data Fiduciary
- D) Unlawful without RBI approval

✅ **Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Processing health data for insurance underwriting and actuarial calculations is the specified purpose for which the customer provides data when applying for a life insurance policy. With proper consent obtained under Section 6, such processing is lawful. The customer provides this data knowing it will be used for risk assessment.

---

**Q9. A payment aggregator processes transaction data of merchants to generate risk scores and then sells these risk scores to third parties for profit. The sale of risk scores is:**

- A) Lawful as risk scores are derived data, not personal data
- B) Lawful if the merchant agreed to general terms of service
- C) Unlawful as selling derived personal data to third parties is not a legitimate use and requires explicit consent
- D) Lawful as payment aggregators are regulated by RBI

✅ **Correct Answer: C**

**Explanation:** Risk scores derived from a merchant's transaction data are personal data as they are identifiable to that merchant. Selling such data to third parties is not a legitimate use under Section 7. Such processing requires explicit, specific consent from the merchant for this commercial purpose.

---

**Q10. A cooperative bank in a rural area processes customer data to help a government agricultural scheme identify eligible beneficiaries. This processing is lawful under:**

- A) Section 6 — customer consent
- B) Section 7(b) — State provision of subsidy, benefit or service
- C) Section 7(i) — employment-related processing
- D) Section 10 — Significant Data Fiduciary obligations

✅ **Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Section 7(b) allows the State and its instrumentalities to process personal data for providing subsidies, benefits, services, certificates, licences or permits. A cooperative bank assisting a government agricultural scheme to identify eligible beneficiaries falls within this legitimate use provision.

# PRINCIPLE 2 — NOTICE & TRANSPARENCY

---

**Q1. A bank sends a customer a 30-page account opening form that contains a one-line data collection clause buried on page 25. Under Rule 3 of DPDP Rules 2025, this notice is:**

- A) Valid as the information is technically present in the document
- B) Valid if the customer signs the form
- C) Invalid as it violates the requirement of being independently understandable
- D) Valid if the RBI approves the form format

✅ **Correct Answer: C**

**Explanation:** Rule 3(a) requires that notice must be presented and be understandable independently of any other information. A one-line clause buried in a 30-page form does not meet this standard. The DPDPA requires that notice be standalone, clear, and accessible — not embedded in lengthy documentation.

---

**Q2. An insurance company's app collects a customer's location data. The notice for this must include:**

- A) Only the fact that location data is collected
- B) An itemised description of what location data is collected and the specific purpose for which it is used
- C) A general statement that "various data may be collected for service improvement"
- D) Only the link to the privacy policy

✅ **Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Rule 3(b)(i) and (ii) require the notice to include an itemised description of the personal data being collected and the specified purpose, along with specific description of goods or services to be provided through such processing. Generic statements do not meet the itemised description requirement.

---

**Q3. A customer opened a bank account before DPDPA came into force. After the Act's commencement, the bank must:**

- A) Immediately close the account and seek fresh account opening
- B) Give the customer a fresh notice describing the data processed and the purpose, as soon as reasonably practicable
- C) Do nothing as pre-existing banking relationships are exempt
- D) Get RBI to notify the customer on its behalf

✅ **Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Section 5(2) specifically addresses pre-existing consents. The bank may continue processing the customer's data but must, as soon as reasonably practicable, give the customer a notice informing her of the personal data processed, the purpose, how to exercise rights, and how to complain to the Board.

---

**Q4. A health insurance policy renewal notice contains data processing information only in English. The policyholder is a Tamil-speaking customer who requested Tamil communication. Under DPDPA, this notice is:**

- A) Valid as English is the official language of business
- B) Invalid as DPDPA requires the option to receive notice in Tamil, which is in the Eighth Schedule
- C) Valid if the insurer's policy documents are approved by IRDAI in English
- D) Valid as the customer can use a translator

✅ **Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Section 5(3) requires the Data Fiduciary to give the Data Principal the option to access the contents of the notice in English or any language specified in the Eighth Schedule to the Constitution. Tamil is in the Eighth Schedule. The customer must be given the option of accessing the notice in Tamil.

---

**Q5. A bank's notice to customers mentions data will be used for "banking services" without specifying what services or what data will be processed. Under Rule 3, this notice is:**

- A) Valid as banking services is a well-understood term
- B) Valid if supplemented by a detailed privacy policy link
- C) Invalid as it does not provide an itemised description of data and specific purpose
- D) Valid if approved by the bank's Board of Directors

✅ **Correct Answer: C**

**Explanation:** Rule 3(b) requires the notice to give an itemised description of the personal data and the specified purpose or purposes, along with specific description of goods or services to be provided. "Banking services" is too vague and does not meet the itemised specificity requirement of Rule 3.

---

**Q6. Under Rule 3(c), a bank's notice must include information through which the customer can:**

- A) Apply for new banking products
- B) Withdraw consent, exercise rights under the Act, and make a complaint to the Board
- C) Check loan eligibility
- D) Contact the bank's customer service for general queries

✅ **Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Rule 3(c) specifically requires the notice to give the particular communication link and other means by which the Data Principal may: (i) withdraw consent, (ii) exercise rights under the Act, and (iii) make a complaint to the Board. This is a mandatory transparency requirement.

---

**Q7. An insurer's chatbot collects customer medical information for a health policy quote. The chatbot does not provide any notice before collecting data. This violates:**

- A) Only the Consent principle
- B) Section 5 — the notice must accompany or precede any request for data
- C) Only the Security principle
- D) Section 10 — Significant Data Fiduciary obligations

✅ **Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Section 5(1) requires that every request for consent shall be accompanied or preceded by a notice. Collecting medical information through a chatbot without first providing a notice about what data is being collected and for what purpose directly violates Section 5(1) of DPDPA.

---

**Q8. A bank updates its data processing practices to include sharing data with a new credit bureau. The existing customers were not informed of this change. Under DPDPA, the bank must:**

- A) Update its privacy policy and assume customers have read it
- B) Provide fresh notice to customers about the new processing activity and purpose
- C) Only inform customers who specifically ask about data sharing
- D) Inform RBI and let RBI notify customers

✅ **Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Adding a new processing activity (sharing with a new credit bureau) constitutes a new purpose beyond what was originally noticed. Under Section 5, the notice must be given before processing for any new purpose. Customers must be specifically informed of this new data sharing, not just through an updated privacy policy.

---

**Q9. A mutual fund company collects investor data through a paper KYC form and later digitizes it. Under DPDPA, is this processing covered?**

- A) No, DPDPA only applies to data collected digitally
- B) Yes, DPDPA applies to personal data collected in non-digital form and digitised subsequently
- C) No, KYC data is exempt from DPDPA
- D) Yes, but only after the investor's written consent for digitization

✅ **Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Section 3(a)(ii) specifically states that the Act applies to processing of digital personal data within the territory of India where the personal data is collected in non-digital form and digitised subsequently. Paper KYC forms that are scanned and digitized therefore fall under DPDPA.

---

**Q10. A bank's notice for a credit card application says "we may share your data with our partners." Under Rule 3, this notice is:**

- A) Valid as it discloses data sharing
- B) Invalid as "partners" is vague — notice must specify the purposes and types of sharing clearly
- C) Valid if the credit card agreement mentions specific partners
- D) Valid if the bank has an IRDAI-approved data sharing policy

✅ **Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Rule 3(b) requires specific and clear language. "We may share with our partners" is vague and does not give the customer fair account of the details necessary to give specific and informed consent. The notice must clearly describe what data is shared, with whom, and for what specific purpose.

# PRINCIPLE 3 — CONSENT

---

**Q1. A bank account opening form has a pre-ticked checkbox saying "I consent to receiving marketing communications." Under DPDPA, this consent is:**

- A) Valid as the customer can untick the box
- B) Valid if it is clearly visible on the form
- C) Invalid as it is not a clear affirmative action — consent must be unambiguous
- D) Valid if the bank is regulated by RBI

✅ **Correct Answer: C**

**Explanation:** Section 6(1) requires consent to be "unambiguous with a clear affirmative action." A pre-ticked checkbox is not a clear affirmative action — the customer has not actively chosen to consent to marketing. This form of consent does not meet the DPDPA standard regardless of whether the bank is regulated.

---

**Q2. An insurance company asks a customer to sign a single consent form for: (i) processing data to issue the policy, and (ii) waiving her right to complain to the Data Protection Board. The consent for (ii) is:**

- A) Valid as it is a contractual agreement
- B) Valid if IRDAI has approved the policy form
- C) Invalid as waiving the right to complain to the Board infringes the Act
- D) Valid if the customer is made aware of the waiver

✅ **Correct Answer: C**

**Explanation:** Section 6(2) states that any part of consent that constitutes an infringement of the provisions of the Act is invalid. The right to complain to the Board is a statutory right under Section 13(3). This right cannot be waived through a contractual consent clause, making such consent invalid to that extent.

---

**Q3. A customer tells her bank that she wants to withdraw consent for the processing of her data for marketing purposes. The bank must:**

- A) Process the withdrawal request within 6 months
- B) Withdraw consent only after the customer completes a notarized declaration
- C) Within a reasonable time, cease and cause its Data Processors to cease marketing-related processing
- D) Continue marketing processing for the remainder of the contractual year

✅ **Correct Answer: C**

**Explanation:** Section 6(6) requires that upon withdrawal of consent, the Data Fiduciary shall within a reasonable time cease and cause its Data Processors to cease processing the personal data for that purpose. There is no provision for delay conditions like end of contractual year or notarized declaration.

---

**Q4. An NBFC bundles consent for a loan with consent for marketing communications, stating both are mandatory to avail the loan. Under DPDPA, this consent for marketing is:**

- A) Valid as the customer chose to take the loan
- B) Valid if NBFC is a licensed entity
- C) Invalid as consent must be free and unconditional — bundling makes marketing consent coercive
- D) Valid as long as the customer was informed of both requirements

✅ **Correct Answer: C**

**Explanation:** Section 6(1) requires consent to be "free" and "unconditional." Conditioning the grant of a loan on consent for marketing makes the marketing consent coercive — it is not freely given. Such bundled consent violates the freeness and unconditionality requirements of valid consent.

---

**Q5. A customer withdraws her consent from an insurance company for processing her health data. The insurance company has an active medical claim of the customer under process. Regarding the active claim, the company:**

- A) Must immediately stop all processing including the claim
- B) May continue processing for the active claim as withdrawal does not affect legality of prior processing and ongoing legal obligations
- C) Must refund the premium and close the policy
- D) Must seek fresh consent specifically for the active claim processing

✅ **Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Section 6(5) states that withdrawal does not affect the legality of processing based on consent before its withdrawal. Additionally, processing an active claim may also be a legitimate use under Section 7 (legal obligation). The insurer may continue processing for the active claim while ceasing processing for other purposes.

---

**Q6. Under Section 6(10), in a Board proceeding, a bank claims it had obtained customer consent for a data sharing practice. Who bears the burden of proving this?**

- A) The customer (Data Principal)
- B) The Data Protection Board
- C) The bank (Data Fiduciary)
- D) The RBI as the banking regulator

✅ **Correct Answer: C**

**Explanation:** Section 6(10) explicitly states that where consent is the basis of processing and a question arises in a proceeding, the Data Fiduciary shall be obliged to prove that notice was given and consent was given by the Data Principal. The bank must produce evidence of valid consent — the burden is on it, not the customer.

---

**Q7. A customer wishes to manage, review and withdraw her consent given to multiple banks through a single platform. Under DPDPA, this can be done through:**

- A) The Data Protection Board's online portal
- B) A Consent Manager registered with the Board
- C) The Ministry of Electronics and IT's DigiLocker platform
- D) The RBI's Banking Ombudsman portal

✅ **Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Section 6(7) and 6(8) allow a Data Principal to give, manage, review or withdraw consent through a Consent Manager. Section 6(9) requires Consent Managers to be registered with the Board. A Consent Manager provides a single interoperable platform for managing consent across multiple Data Fiduciaries.

---

**Q8. A life insurance policy application requires a customer's medical history. The consent for collecting this data must be:**

- A) General — covering all possible medical conditions
- B) Specific — for the defined purpose of life insurance underwriting and risk assessment
- C) Permanent and irrevocable once given for the policy period
- D) Witnessed by a doctor for medical data

✅ **Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Section 6(1) requires consent to be "specific" — for a defined, stated purpose. The consent for collecting medical history must be specifically for life insurance underwriting and risk assessment. General or open-ended consent for "all insurance purposes" would not meet the specificity requirement.

---

**Q9. A bank's mobile app requires customers to provide consent for ALL data collection as a single option, without the ability to give consent selectively for different types of processing. Under DPDPA, this is:**

- A) Valid as it is simpler for the customer
- B) Valid if the app's privacy settings can be changed later
- C) Invalid as consent must be specific for each purpose, not a single blanket consent
- D) Valid if approved by the bank's data governance committee

✅ **Correct Answer: C**

**Explanation:** Section 6(1) requires consent to be "specific" — tied to a specified purpose. Bundling all types of data collection into a single all-or-nothing consent does not meet the specificity requirement. Customers must be able to give consent for specific purposes, not just a blanket "accept all."

---

**Q10. An insurance agent verbally assures a customer that signing the consent form is "just a formality" and does not affect any rights. The customer signs under this impression. Is this valid consent?**

- A) Valid as the customer voluntarily signed the document
- B) Invalid as the consent is not "informed" — the customer was misled about its effect
- C) Valid if witnessed by a third party
- D) Valid as the agent acted in good faith

✅ **Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Section 6(1) requires consent to be "informed" — the customer must understand what she is consenting to. If an agent misrepresents the nature of a consent form, the resulting consent is not informed. Consent obtained through misleading representations does not meet the "informed" requirement of Section 6(1).

# PRINCIPLE 4 — PURPOSE LIMITATION

---

**Q1. A bank collects a customer's income and employment data for a personal loan. After the loan is disbursed, it uses this data to push-sell mutual fund products. This use is:**

- A) Lawful as it is within the same bank
- B) Lawful if disclosed in the bank's master consent form
- C) Unlawful as it exceeds the specified purpose of loan processing
- D) Lawful as financial products are all within the same sector

✅ **Correct Answer: C**

**Explanation:** The specified purpose for collecting income and employment data was personal loan processing. Using it for cross-selling mutual funds is a completely different purpose. Section 6(1) and 8(7) require data to be used only for the specified purpose. Fresh consent is required for the cross-selling purpose.

---

**Q2. An insurance company collects customer health data to process a mediclaim. It later uses the same data to deny renewal of a life insurance policy. This cross-use is:**

- A) Lawful as both are insurance products
- B) Lawful if IRDAI permits such use
- C) Unlawful as health data collected for mediclaim processing cannot be used for life insurance underwriting without a new specified purpose and consent
- D) Lawful as the insurer owns the data once collected

✅ **Correct Answer: C**

**Explanation:** The specified purpose for collecting health data was mediclaim processing. Using it for life insurance underwriting decisions is a different purpose. Under Section 6(1) and 8(7), processing must be limited to the specified purpose. Cross-using data for a different product without fresh consent violates purpose limitation.

---

**Q3. A bank's loan repayment data of a customer is shared with a credit bureau under the Credit Information Companies (Regulation) Act. This is:**

- A) Unlawful as it shares data beyond the original loan purpose
- B) Lawful as it is mandated by law — a legal obligation overrides purpose limitation
- C) Unlawful without specific customer consent for credit bureau sharing
- D) Lawful only for non-performing assets

✅ **Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Section 7(d) allows processing for fulfilling a legal obligation to disclose information to the State or its instrumentalities. Sharing credit data with regulated credit bureaus under the Credit Information Companies (Regulation) Act is a statutory obligation that overrides the strict purpose limitation principle.

---

**Q4. A customer provides her email address to a bank for receiving account statements. The bank starts sending promotional offers from its insurance subsidiary to that email. This is:**

- A) Lawful as it is for the bank's own group companies
- B) Lawful if the bank's conglomerate has a unified data sharing policy
- C) Unlawful as the email was collected for the purpose of account statements, not promotional communications
- D) Lawful as email communication is a minor use of data

✅ **Correct Answer: C**

**Explanation:** The customer's email was provided for the specific purpose of receiving account statements. Using it for promotional communications — even from group companies — is a different purpose. Section 6(1) and 8(7) prohibit using data beyond the specified purpose without fresh consent from the customer.

---

**Q5. After a home loan is fully repaid, the bank continues retaining the customer's property and income documents for future cross-selling opportunities. Under DPDPA, this retention is:**

- A) Lawful as the bank may offer the customer future products
- B) Lawful if the documents are stored securely
- C) Unlawful as the specified purpose (home loan processing) has been served and data must be erased
- D) Lawful as long as the bank does not misuse the data

✅ **Correct Answer: C**

**Explanation:** Section 8(7) requires data to be erased as soon as the specified purpose is no longer being served. After the home loan is fully repaid, the loan processing purpose is served. Retaining documents for future cross-selling is not the original specified purpose, making further retention unlawful (subject to any applicable legal retention requirements).

---

**Q6. A health insurer uses policyholder data to conduct internal research on disease trends. The research improves actuarial models. Is this use lawful?**

- A) Yes, as it is for business improvement
- B) Only if the data is anonymized and research meets standards under Section 17(2)(b) and Second Schedule
- C) Yes, as insurers are exempt from purpose limitation
- D) No, research use of insurance data is always prohibited

✅ **Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Section 17(2)(b) exempts processing for research, archiving or statistical purposes if carried on in accordance with the standards specified in the Second Schedule of DPDP Rules 2025. The research must not be used to make decisions specific to any individual Data Principal and must meet the prescribed standards.

---

**Q7. A payment bank collects UPI transaction data to process payments. It later sells anonymized transaction patterns to advertisers. The anonymized data sale is:**

- A) Always lawful as anonymized data is not personal data
- B) Potentially unlawful if the "anonymized" data can re-identify individuals — purpose limitation still applies
- C) Lawful as selling data to advertisers is industry practice
- D) Lawful if RBI approves such data monetization

✅ **Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Purpose limitation under DPDPA requires data to be used for the specified purpose of processing payments. Selling transaction patterns to advertisers is a completely different purpose requiring fresh consent. Additionally, if data claimed to be anonymized can still re-identify individuals, it remains personal data under Section 2(t) of DPDPA.

---

**Q8. A non-banking financial company (NBFC) collects vehicle RC documents for a vehicle loan. After loan closure, RTO regulations require it to retain vehicle-related documents for 7 years. Under DPDPA:**

- A) The NBFC must erase the documents immediately upon loan closure
- B) The NBFC may retain the documents for 7 years as legal retention requirement overrides DPDPA erasure
- C) The NBFC must seek fresh consent to retain the documents post loan closure
- D) The NBFC must transfer the documents to the customer and then erase

✅ **Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Section 8(7) contains an explicit exception — data need not be erased if "retention is necessary for compliance with any law for the time being in force." If RTO regulations mandate 7-year retention, the NBFC may retain vehicle documents for that period without violating DPDPA's erasure obligation.

---

**Q9. A bank provides credit card data to a fraud analytics company for detecting fraudulent transactions. After the fraud investigation is complete, the analytics company continues using this data to build commercial fraud prediction products. This continued use is:**

- A) Lawful as fraud analytics is always in public interest
- B) Unlawful as the specified purpose (fraud investigation) is served and commercial use requires fresh consent
- C) Lawful if the analytics company anonymizes the data
- D) Lawful if the bank approved the commercial use in the contract

✅ **Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** The specified purpose for sharing the data was fraud detection for a specific investigation. Once the investigation is complete, the purpose is served. Using the data for building commercial products is a new and different purpose. Neither the bank's approval in the contract nor anonymization removes the DPDPA purpose limitation obligation.

---

**Q10. A bank uses a customer's dormant account data (no transactions for 2 years) to offer targeted banking products. Under DPDPA, this use:**

- A) Is lawful as the customer is still a customer of the bank
- B) Is lawful if the bank sends a notice with the marketing offer
- C) May be unlawful if the specified purpose for dormant account data has been deemed no longer served under Section 8(8)
- D) Is always lawful for account holders regardless of account activity

✅ **Correct Answer: C**

**Explanation:** Section 8(8) provides that the purpose is deemed no longer served when the Data Principal has neither approached the Fiduciary for the specified purpose nor exercised any rights for the prescribed period. A dormant account may trigger the deemed expiry provision under Rule 8, after which further processing including marketing may be unlawful.

# PRINCIPLE 5 — DATA MINIMIZATION

---

**Q1. A bank's mobile loan app requires customers to mandatorily submit: (i) PAN card, (ii) Aadhaar, (iii) salary slips, (iv) full social media login credentials. Regarding (iv), this is:**

- A) Acceptable as it helps assess creditworthiness
- B) A violation of data minimization as social media credentials are not necessary for loan processing
- C) Acceptable if the customer voluntarily provides them
- D) Acceptable if the bank's credit risk policy requires it

✅ **Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Section 6(1) limits consent and collection to personal data "necessary for such specified purpose." Social media login credentials are not necessary for loan processing — PAN, Aadhaar, and salary slips are standard requirements. Collecting unnecessary data violates the data minimization principle regardless of the bank's internal policy.

---

**Q2. An insurance company collects a comprehensive health questionnaire for a term life insurance policy including: current health conditions, family medical history, smoking habits. Additionally, it also collects the customer's daily food habits and exercise routine. The food and exercise data collection is:**

- A) Justified for accurate mortality risk assessment
- B) A violation of data minimization as daily food and exercise habits are excessive for term life underwriting
- C) Justified if the policy offers wellness discounts
- D) Acceptable as health insurers are exempt from data minimization

✅ **Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** While current health, family history, and smoking habits are standard and necessary for life insurance underwriting, daily food habits and exercise routines go beyond what is necessary for the specified purpose. Data minimization under Section 6(1) requires limiting collection to what is necessary — granular lifestyle data exceeds this threshold for basic term life insurance.

---

**Q1. A customer closes her savings account with a bank. RBI regulations require the bank to maintain account records for 10 years post closure. Under DPDPA, the bank:**

**Explanation:** Section 8(7) contains the exception that data need not be erased where "retention is necessary for compliance with any law for the time being in force." RBI regulations mandating 10-year retention of banking records constitute such a legal requirement, which overrides DPDPA's general erasure obligation upon account closure.

**Q2. Under Rule 8(3) of DPDP Rules 2025, what is the minimum period for which a bank must retain personal data and processing logs from a transaction date?**

- A) 6 months
- B) 1 year
- C) 3 years
- D) 5 years

✅ **Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Rule 8(3) requires that without prejudice to other erasure obligations, a Data Fiduciary shall retain personal data, associated traffic data and other logs of processing for a minimum period of one year from the date of processing. ✅ **Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Section 8(7) requires erasure unless retention is necessary for compliance with any law. IRDAI's 7-year requirement sets the legal maximum. Retaining data for 15 years for "business analytics" goes beyond legal necessity. Once the 7-year period under IRDAI regulations ends, further retention for analytics violates storage limitation under DPDPA.

This is a mandatory pre-erasure notification requirement.

AI loan approval algorithms that could discriminate or violate rights must be assessed.

- D) A retired government official with data governance experience

✅ **Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Section 10(2)(a) specifies that the DPO must: represent the Significant Data Fiduciary under the Act, be based in India, be an individual responsible to the Board of Directors or similar governing body, and be the point of contact for grievance redressal. The requirement to be "based in India" and "responsible to the Board of Directors" are key accountability requirements.

**Q5. A bank (Data Fiduciary) engages a marketing analytics firm (Data Processor) through a verbal agreement to process customer transaction data. Under Section 8(2), this arrangement is:**

- A) Valid if the firm has a strong industry reputation
- B) Invalid — a Data Processor can only be engaged under a valid written contract
- C) Valid if the engagement is for less than 3 months
- D) Valid if both parties sign a Non-Disclosure Agreement

✅ **Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Section 8(2) explicitly states that a Data Fiduciary may engage a Data Processor only under a valid contract. A verbal agreement or informal arrangement does not constitute a valid contract for this purpose. The engagement without a valid contract violates Section 8(2) and undermines the bank's accountability framework.

**Q6. A fintech company (Significant Data Fiduciary) completes its Data Protection Impact Assessment. Under Rule 13(2), the DPIA report must be furnished to the Board by:**

- A) The Chief Data Officer of the company
- B) The Data Protection Officer of the company
- C) The person who carried out the DPIA and audit
- D) The CEO of the company on behalf of the Board of Directors

✅ **Correct Answer: C**

**Explanation:** Rule 13(2) requires the Significant Data Fiduciary to cause the person carrying out the Data Protection Impact Assessment and audit to furnish to the Board a report containing significant observations. This ensures independence — the assessor reports directly to the Board, not through company management, maintaining the integrity of the accountability mechanism.

**Q7. A bank's customer grievance about data processing remains unresolved for 95 days. Under Rule 14(3), this is:**

- A) Acceptable as banking grievances require thorough investigation
- B) A violation — grievances must be resolved within a reasonable period not exceeding 90 days
- C) Acceptable if the grievance involves a complex data breach
- D) Acceptable if RBI's 120-day limit for banking grievances applies instead

✅ **Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Rule 14(3) requires every Data Fiduciary to have a grievance redressal system that resolves grievances within a reasonable period not exceeding ninety days. 95 days exceeds this limit. The bank must implement appropriate technical and organizational measures to ensure effectiveness of the system within the 90-day window.

**Q8. An insurance company is being assessed for notification as a Significant Data Fiduciary. Which factor would NOT be considered by the Central Government under Section 10(1)?**

- A) Volume and sensitivity of personal data processed
- B) Risk to electoral democracy
- C) The company's stock market listing status
- D) Potential impact on sovereignty and integrity of India

✅ **Correct Answer: C**

**Explanation:** Section 10(1) lists the factors for assessing Significant Data Fiduciary status: volume and sensitivity of data, risk to Data Principal rights, potential impact on sovereignty and integrity, risk to electoral democracy, security of the State, and public order. Stock market listing status is not a consideration — the assessment is based on data processing impact, not corporate structure.

**Q9. Under Section 8(4), a bank's accountability obligation requires implementing:**

- A) A dedicated DPDPA compliance department with 50+ employees
- B) Appropriate technical and organisational measures to ensure effective observance of DPDPA
- C) Board-level resolution approving DPDPA compliance annually
- D) DPDPA compliance certification from a government-approved body

✅ **Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Section 8(4) requires a Data Fiduciary to implement appropriate technical and organisational measures to ensure effective observance of the Act and rules. This is a broad accountability obligation requiring systemic, proactive measures. The Act does not prescribe a specific number of employees, board resolutions, or certification requirements — the measures must be appropriate to the fiduciary's scale and risk.

**Q10. A bank's Data Protection Officer (DPO) reports a potential DPDPA violation to the bank's CEO who instructs the DPO not to report it to the Data Protection Board. Under DPDPA, the DPO's primary accountability is to:**

- A) Follow the CEO's instructions as the CEO is the highest authority
- B) The Board of Directors of the bank — the DPO is responsible to the governing body, not the CEO
- C) The Reserve Bank of India as the banking regulator
- D) The Ministry of Finance as the banking sector regulator

✅ **Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Section 10(2)(a)(iii) specifies that the DPO must be "an individual responsible to the Board of Directors or similar governing body of the Significant Data Fiduciary." The DPO's accountability runs to the Board of Directors, not to the CEO or management. This independence ensures the DPO can report compliance issues without management pressure, maintaining accountability integrity.
