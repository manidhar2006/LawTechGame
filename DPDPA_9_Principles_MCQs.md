# PRINCIPLE 1 — LAWFUL PROCESSING

## 📖 Explanation

**Key Section: Section 4 of DPDPA 2023**

Lawful Processing is the foundational principle of the DPDPA. It establishes that **no person may process the personal data of a Data Principal unless it is done lawfully**. Processing is considered lawful only when:

1. **The Data Principal has given her consent**, OR
2. **Processing is for "certain legitimate uses"** as specified under Section 7

The Act defines **"lawful purpose"** as *any purpose which is not expressly forbidden by law*. This is a broad but important definition — it means organizations must ensure their data processing activities do not violate any existing law, whether the IT Act, consumer protection laws, or any other applicable legislation.

### Grounds for Lawful Processing (Section 7 — Certain Legitimate Uses):
- Provision of subsidies, benefits, services, certificates, licences or permits by the State
- Performance of State functions under any law
- Compliance with legal obligations
- Compliance with court orders/decrees
- Medical emergencies
- Epidemic or public health threats
- Disaster management
- Employment-related purposes

### Key Takeaway:
> Without either **consent** or a **legitimate use** under Section 7, processing of personal data is unlawful under DPDPA, regardless of any other justification.

## ❓ MCQs — Principle 1: Lawful Processing

---

**Q1. Under Section 4 of DPDPA 2023, a person may process personal data of a Data Principal only if:**

- A) The data is publicly available
- B) The processing is for commercial purposes
- C) The Data Principal has given consent or the processing is for certain legitimate uses
- D) The Data Fiduciary has a privacy policy in place

✅ **Correct Answer: C**

**Explanation:** Section 4 clearly states that personal data may be processed only (a) with the consent of the Data Principal, or (b) for certain legitimate uses as referred to in Section 7. Having a privacy policy or commercial purpose alone does not make processing lawful.

---

**Q2. The term "lawful purpose" under Section 4 of DPDPA is defined as:**

- A) A purpose approved by the Data Protection Board
- B) Any purpose which is not expressly forbidden by law
- C) A purpose for which the government has given permission
- D) Any purpose related to national security

✅ **Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** The Act explicitly defines "lawful purpose" as any purpose which is not expressly forbidden by law. This is a broad definition and does not require prior government approval or Board sanction.

---

**Q3. Which of the following is NOT a ground for "certain legitimate uses" under Section 7 of DPDPA?**

- A) Medical emergency involving threat to life
- B) Processing for a Data Fiduciary's marketing campaign
- C) Compliance with a court order
- D) Disaster management purposes

✅ **Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Section 7 lists specific legitimate uses such as medical emergencies, court orders, and disaster management. Marketing campaigns are NOT listed as a legitimate use and would require explicit consent of the Data Principal to be lawful.

---

**Q4. X, a company, processes personal data of its users without their consent, claiming it is for "business improvement." Under DPDPA, this processing is:**

- A) Lawful because it is for business improvement
- B) Lawful if a privacy policy mentions it
- C) Unlawful because business improvement is not a legitimate use under Section 7
- D) Lawful if done anonymously

✅ **Correct Answer: C**

**Explanation:** "Business improvement" is not listed as a legitimate use under Section 7. Without consent or a recognized legitimate use, processing personal data is unlawful under Section 4 of DPDPA.

---

**Q5. A pregnant woman enrolls on a government app to avail maternity benefits and consents to provide her personal data. The government then uses this data to determine her eligibility for another prescribed benefit. This is:**

- A) Unlawful as fresh consent is needed for each benefit
- B) Lawful under Section 7(b) as a legitimate use by the State
- C) Unlawful as it violates purpose limitation
- D) Lawful only if she is informed in advance

✅ **Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** This is the exact illustration provided in Section 7(b) of the Act. The State may process personal data for providing prescribed subsidies, benefits, and services, and where she has previously consented for any benefit, the State may use that data for determining eligibility for other prescribed benefits.

---

**Q6. Under which section of DPDPA are the "certain legitimate uses" for processing personal data without consent listed?**

- A) Section 5
- B) Section 6
- C) Section 7
- D) Section 8

✅ **Correct Answer: C**

**Explanation:** Section 7 of the DPDPA specifically lists all "certain legitimate uses" for which personal data can be processed without requiring explicit consent from the Data Principal. Section 5 deals with notice, Section 6 with consent, and Section 8 with general obligations.

---

**Q7. Which of the following scenarios represents LAWFUL processing under DPDPA without explicit consent?**

- A) An e-commerce company selling user data to advertisers
- B) A hospital processing patient data during a declared epidemic
- C) A social media platform using data for targeted advertising
- D) A company sharing employee data with a competitor

✅ **Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Section 7(g) specifically allows processing personal data for medical treatment or health services during an epidemic or outbreak of disease. This is a recognized legitimate use that does not require explicit consent.

---

**Q8. A Data Fiduciary processes personal data for compliance with a judgment of a civil court in India. This processing is:**

- A) Unlawful as courts cannot authorize data processing
- B) Lawful under Section 7(e) as compliance with court orders is a legitimate use
- C) Lawful only if the Data Principal consents
- D) Unlawful unless approved by the Data Protection Board

✅ **Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Section 7(e) specifically recognizes compliance with any judgment, decree or order issued under any law in force in India as a legitimate use for processing personal data, making such processing lawful even without the Data Principal's explicit consent.

---

**Q9. Processing of personal data for prevention of corporate espionage by an employer falls under which category of DPDPA?**

- A) Unlawful processing as it violates employee privacy
- B) Lawful processing under Section 7(i) — employment-related purposes
- C) Lawful only with written consent of the employee
- D) Unlawful unless directed by the Central Government

✅ **Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Section 7(i) specifically allows processing for employment purposes, including safeguarding the employer from loss or liability such as prevention of corporate espionage, maintenance of confidentiality of trade secrets and intellectual property.

---

**Q10. Which of the following statements about lawful processing under DPDPA is INCORRECT?**

- A) Processing must be either consented to or fall under legitimate uses
- B) A lawful purpose means any purpose not expressly forbidden by law
- C) Any processing done by a private company is automatically lawful
- D) State instrumentalities can process data for national security purposes

✅ **Correct Answer: C**

**Explanation:** Processing by a private company is NOT automatically lawful. It must still be based on consent or fall under one of the legitimate uses listed in Section 7. The mere fact that the processor is a private entity does not grant automatic lawfulness to data processing activities.

# PRINCIPLE 2 — NOTICE & TRANSPARENCY

## 📖 Explanation

**Key Sections: Section 5 of DPDPA 2023 + Rule 3 of DPDP Rules 2025**

The Notice & Transparency principle requires that **Data Fiduciaries must inform Data Principals clearly and completely** about the processing of their personal data — before or at the time of seeking consent.

### What must a Notice contain? (Section 5 + Rule 3)

1. **Description of personal data** being collected (itemised)
2. **Specific purpose** of processing
3. **Description of goods/services** to be provided through such processing
4. How to **exercise rights** under the Act
5. How to **withdraw consent**
6. How to **complain to the Board**
7. **Communication link** to access the Data Fiduciary's website or app
8. **Contact information** of the person who can answer questions

### Key Rules under Rule 3:
- Notice must be presented **independently** — not buried in terms & conditions
- Must be in **clear and plain language**
- Withdrawal of consent must be as **easy as giving consent**
- Notice must include a **specific communication link** to the Data Fiduciary's platform

### Pre-existing Consent (Section 5(2)):
Where a Data Principal gave consent **before the Act came into force**, the Data Fiduciary must:
- Give a fresh notice as soon as reasonably practicable
- Describe data collected and purpose
- The Data Fiduciary may continue processing until consent is withdrawn

### Language:
- Notice must be available in **English OR any language specified in the Eighth Schedule** to the Constitution

## ❓ MCQs — Principle 2: Notice & Transparency

---

**Q1. Under DPDPA 2023, a notice given to the Data Principal must be presented:**

- A) Along with the terms and conditions document
- B) Independently and understandably separate from any other information
- C) Only after the data has been collected
- D) Only in the English language

✅ **Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Rule 3(a) specifically states that the notice must be presented and be understandable independently of any other information that has been, is, or may be made available by the Data Fiduciary. Embedding notice in terms and conditions would violate this requirement.

---

**Q2. Which of the following is NOT required to be included in a notice under Section 5 and Rule 3 of DPDPA?**

- A) Description of personal data being collected
- B) Purpose of processing
- C) The financial details of the Data Fiduciary
- D) How to withdraw consent

✅ **Correct Answer: C**

**Explanation:** Section 5 and Rule 3 require the notice to include description of personal data, purpose of processing, rights of the Data Principal, and how to withdraw consent and complain. Financial details of the Data Fiduciary are not required to be disclosed in the notice.

---

**Q3. Under DPDPA, in what language must the notice and consent request be presented to the Data Principal?**

- A) Only in English
- B) Only in Hindi
- C) In English or any language specified in the Eighth Schedule to the Constitution
- D) In the official language of the state where the Data Fiduciary is registered

✅ **Correct Answer: C**

**Explanation:** Section 5(3) and Section 6(3) both require that the Data Fiduciary must give the Data Principal the option to access the contents of the notice in English or any language specified in the Eighth Schedule to the Constitution of India.

---

**Q4. X, an individual, gave consent to an e-commerce company Y before the DPDPA came into force. After the Act's commencement, Y must:**

- A) Immediately stop processing X's data and seek fresh consent
- B) Give X a notice as soon as reasonably practicable describing the data and purpose
- C) Delete all of X's data and start fresh
- D) Do nothing as prior consent is permanently valid

✅ **Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Section 5(2) deals with pre-existing consents. The Data Fiduciary may continue processing but must, as soon as reasonably practicable, give the Data Principal a notice describing the personal data processed and the purpose. The Data Fiduciary may continue until the Data Principal withdraws consent.

**Q5. The ease of withdrawing consent under DPDPA must be:**

✅ **Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Section 6(4) and Rule 3(c)(i) both state that the ease of withdrawing consent shall be comparable to the ease with which such consent was given. If consent was given with a single click, withdrawal must also be available with a similarly simple process.

**Q6. Under Rule 3 of DPDP Rules 2025, a notice must include which of the following regarding the Data Fiduciary's platform?**

✅ **Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Rule 3(c) requires that the notice must give the particular communication link for accessing the website or app, or both, of the Data Fiduciary, through which the Data Principal can withdraw consent, exercise rights, and make complaints to the Board.

**Q7. Y, a bank, asks X to open an account via video-based KYC. According to the illustration in Section 5(1), Y must:**

- A) Collect data first, then provide notice

- C) Provide notice only after the KYC is complete
- D) Provide notice only if X requests it

✅ **Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** The illustration under Section 5(1) specifically addresses this scenario. Y (the bank) shall accompany or precede the request for personal data with a notice to X, describing the personal data and the purpose of its processing. Notice must come before or with the request, not after.

**Q8. Which Rule of DPDP Rules 2025 governs the notice to be given by a Data Fiduciary to a Data Principal?**

- A) Rule 2
- B) Rule 3
- C) Rule 5
- D) Rule 7

✅ **Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Rule 3 of the Digital Personal Data Protection Rules 2025 specifically governs the "Notice given by Data Fiduciary to Data Principal" and sets out all the requirements for what a valid notice must contain and how it must be presented.

---

**Q9. According to Rule 3, the description of personal data in the notice must be:**

**Explanation:** Rule 3(a) requires that the notice must be "presented and be understandable independently of any other information." Burying the notice in a lengthy terms and conditions document directly violates this requirement, making such a notice invalid under DPDPA.

# PRINCIPLE 3 — CONSENT

## 📖 Explanation

**Key Section: Section 6 of DPDPA 2023**

Consent is one of the two legal bases for processing personal data under DPDPA. When legitimate use under Section 7 is not applicable, **valid consent is mandatory**.

### The 5 Attributes of Valid Consent (Section 6(1)):

| Attribute | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Free** | No coercion, undue influence or fear |
| **Specific** | For a defined, stated purpose only |
| **Informed** | Based on a proper notice given under Section 5 |
| **Unconditional** | No conditions or bundling attached |
| **Unambiguous** | A clear, affirmative action — not silence or pre-ticking |

### Additional Rules on Consent:
- Must be **limited to data necessary** for the specified purpose (data minimization)
- Any part of consent that **violates the Act or any law is invalid** to that extent
- Data Principal has the right to **withdraw consent at any time**
- Withdrawal must be as **easy** as giving consent
- **Consequences of withdrawal** are borne by the Data Principal
- Withdrawal does **not affect legality** of processing done before withdrawal
- Consent can be managed through a **Consent Manager** registered with the Board
- In a proceeding, the **Data Fiduciary bears the burden of proving** that notice was given and consent was obtained

### Consent Manager (Section 6(7)-(9)):
- Registered with the Data Protection Board
- Acts as a **single point of contact**
- Enables Data Principal to **give, manage, review and withdraw** consent
- Must be on an **accessible, transparent and interoperable platform**

---

## ❓ MCQs — Principle 3: Consent

---

**Q1. Which of the following is NOT one of the required attributes of valid consent under Section 6(1) of DPDPA?**

- A) Free
- B) Informed
- C) Reversible
- D) Unambiguous

✅ **Correct Answer: C**

**Explanation:** Section 6(1) lists five attributes of valid consent: free, specific, informed, unconditional, and unambiguous. "Reversible" is not one of them. While consent can be withdrawn, the attribute itself is not called "reversible" in the Act.

---

**Q2. X downloads a telemedicine app and gives consent for telemedicine services AND access to her phone contacts. Under DPDPA, the consent for phone contacts is:**

- A) Valid as X freely gave consent
- B) Invalid as phone contacts are not necessary for telemedicine services
- C) Valid if the app has a privacy policy explaining the use of contacts
- D) Valid as long as it is mentioned in the terms of service

✅ **Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** This is the direct illustration from Section 6(1) of the Act. Consent must be limited to personal data necessary for the specified purpose. Since phone contacts are not necessary for telemedicine services, consent for accessing contacts is invalid as it violates the data minimization aspect of consent.

---

**Q3. X buys an insurance policy and gives consent for: (i) processing her data for issuing the policy, and (ii) waiving her right to file a complaint with the Board. What is the status of consent (ii)?**

- A) Valid as X freely agreed to waive the right
- B) Valid if the insurer mentions it clearly in the policy document
- C) Invalid as it constitutes an infringement of the Act
- D) Valid only if approved by the Data Protection Board

✅ **Correct Answer: C**

**Explanation:** This is the exact illustration given in Section 6(2). Any part of consent that constitutes an infringement of the provisions of the Act is invalid to the extent of such infringement. Waiving the right to file a complaint with the Board is a right granted by the Act and cannot be waived through consent.

---

**Q4. When a Data Principal withdraws consent, the legal consequences are:**

- A) All previous processing becomes unlawful
- B) The Data Fiduciary can continue processing for 6 months
- C) The consequences are borne by the Data Principal and withdrawal does not affect legality of prior processing
- D) The Data Fiduciary can seek compensation from the Data Principal

✅ **Correct Answer: C**

**Explanation:** Section 6(5) clearly states that the consequences of withdrawal shall be borne by the Data Principal and such withdrawal shall not affect the legality of processing based on consent before its withdrawal. This means past processing remains lawful even after withdrawal.

---

**Q5. After X withdraws consent from a telecom provider Y, Y must:**

- A) Continue processing until the billing cycle ends
- B) Within a reasonable time, cease and cause its Data Processors to cease processing
- C) Immediately delete all data including backups
- D) Seek fresh consent before ceasing processing

✅ **Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Section 6(6) requires that after withdrawal of consent, the Data Fiduciary shall within a reasonable time cease and cause its Data Processors to cease processing the personal data, unless such processing is required or authorized under any other provision of the Act or any other law.

---

**Q6. A Consent Manager under DPDPA acts as:**

- A) A regulator appointed by the Central Government
- B) A single point of contact enabling Data Principals to give, manage, review and withdraw consent
- C) A lawyer representing the Data Principal in Board proceedings
- D) An auditor verifying Data Fiduciary compliance

✅ **Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Section 2(g) defines a Consent Manager as a person registered with the Board who acts as a single point of contact to enable a Data Principal to give, manage, review and withdraw her consent through an accessible, transparent and interoperable platform.

---

**Q7. In a proceeding before the Data Protection Board, who bears the burden of proving that valid consent was obtained?**

- A) The Data Principal
- B) The Data Protection Board
- C) The Data Fiduciary
- D) The Consent Manager

✅ **Correct Answer: C**

**Explanation:** Section 6(10) explicitly states that where consent is the basis of processing and a question arises in a proceeding, the Data Fiduciary shall be obliged to prove that notice was given and consent was given by the Data Principal in accordance with the provisions of the Act.

---

**Q8. A website uses pre-ticked checkboxes for consent to data processing. Under DPDPA, this consent is:**

- A) Valid if the checkbox is clearly visible
- B) Valid if the user does not untick the box
- C) Invalid as it is not a clear affirmative action
- D) Valid if mentioned in the terms and conditions

✅ **Correct Answer: C**

**Explanation:** Section 6(1) requires consent to be "unambiguous with a clear affirmative action." A pre-ticked checkbox is not a clear affirmative action — the user has not actively chosen to consent. This form of consent does not meet the DPDPA standard.

---

**Q9. X places an order on an e-commerce platform Y and then withdraws consent. Y must stop processing for future orders. However, regarding the already ordered and paid-for goods, Y:**

- A) Must immediately cancel the order

- C) Must refund X and cancel the order
- D) Must seek fresh consent to deliver the goods

✅ **Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** The illustration under Section 6(5) specifically addresses this. If X withdraws consent, Y may stop enabling X to use the app for placing orders but may NOT stop processing for the supply of goods already ordered and paid for by X, as this would affect the legal transaction already completed.

---

**Q10. Under Section 6(9), for a Consent Manager to operate, it must be:**

- A) Approved by the Ministry of Electronics and IT
- B) Registered with the Data Protection Board
- C) Certified by an independent auditor
- D) Approved by the Central Government's Cabinet Committee

✅ **Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Section 6(9) states that every Consent Manager shall be registered with the Board in such manner and subject to such technical, operational, financial and other conditions as may be prescribed. Rule 4 and First Schedule of DPDP Rules 2025 further detail the registration conditions.

# PRINCIPLE 4 — PURPOSE LIMITATION

## 📖 Explanation

**Key Sections: Section 6(1), Section 8(7), Section 8(8)**

Purpose Limitation means that **personal data collected for one specific purpose cannot be used for any other purpose** without fresh consent or a new legitimate use.

### Core Elements:

1. **Specified Purpose** — The purpose must be clearly stated in the notice given to the Data Principal
2. **Bounded Processing** — Processing must not go beyond what was stated in the notice
3. **Erasure When Purpose is Served** — Once the purpose is fulfilled, data must be erased

### When is Purpose Deemed No Longer Served? (Section 8(8)):
The purpose is deemed no longer served when the Data Principal does NOT:
- Approach the Data Fiduciary for performance of the specified purpose, AND
- Exercise any rights in relation to such processing

...for such time period as may be prescribed (see Third Schedule of Rules).

### Data Erasure Obligation (Section 8(7)):
Unless retention is required by law, the Data Fiduciary must:
- **Erase personal data** upon withdrawal of consent, OR
- As soon as it is **reasonable to assume the specified purpose is no longer served**
- **Cause its Data Processors** to also erase the data

### Illustration from the Act:
> X registers on an online marketplace to sell her used car. The marketplace helps complete the sale. Once the sale is concluded, the specified purpose is served and Y shall no longer retain her personal data.

## ❓ MCQs — Principle 4: Purpose Limitation

---

**Q1. The "specified purpose" under DPDPA means:**

- A) Any purpose approved by the Data Protection Board
- B) The purpose mentioned in the notice given by the Data Fiduciary to the Data Principal
- C) Any purpose described in the Data Fiduciary's privacy policy
- D) The purpose approved by the Central Government

✅ **Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Section 2(za) defines "specified purpose" as the purpose mentioned in the notice given by the Data Fiduciary to the Data Principal in accordance with the provisions of the Act and the rules made thereunder. This ties purpose limitation directly to the notice requirement.

---

**Q2. X registers on an online marketplace to sell her used car. After the sale is concluded, the marketplace Y:**

- A) May retain X's data for future marketing
- B) May retain data for 3 years as per standard practice
- C) Shall no longer retain her personal data as the purpose is served
- D) May retain data until X explicitly requests deletion

✅ **Correct Answer: C**

**Explanation:** This is the direct illustration from Section 8(7). Once the specified purpose (selling the car) is served, the Data Fiduciary must erase the personal data. The purpose is no longer being served, triggering the erasure obligation under Section 8(7)(a).

---

**Q3. A bank Y is required by banking law to maintain client identity records for 10 years after account closure. X closes her account. Y must:**

- A) Erase X's data immediately as per DPDPA
- B) Retain X's data for 10 years as required by law
- C) Seek fresh consent to retain the data
- D) Transfer the data to the Data Protection Board

✅ **Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** This is the illustration from Section 8(7). Section 8(7) provides an exception — unless retention is necessary for compliance with any law for the time being in force. Since banking law requires 10-year retention, Y must retain X's data for that period despite account closure.

---

**Q4. Under Section 8(8), the specified purpose is deemed to no longer be served when the Data Principal:**

- A) Sends a written request to erase data
- B) Does not approach the Data Fiduciary for the specified purpose AND does not exercise her rights for the prescribed time period
- C) Stops using the Data Fiduciary's services for 30 days
- D) Submits a complaint to the Data Protection Board

✅ **Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Section 8(8) states that the purpose is deemed no longer served when the Data Principal does not (a) approach the Data Fiduciary for performance of the specified purpose, AND (b) exercise any rights in relation to such processing, for such prescribed time period. Both conditions must be absent.

---

**Q5. An e-commerce company collected your data to process an order. After delivery, it uses your data to send promotional offers. Under DPDPA, this is:**

- A) Lawful as it improves customer experience
- B) Lawful if mentioned in the privacy policy
- C) Unlawful as it exceeds the specified purpose of order processing
- D) Lawful as long as the offers are relevant to your purchase

✅ **Correct Answer: C**

**Explanation:** Purpose limitation requires that data collected for order processing can only be used for that purpose. Using the data for promotional marketing exceeds the specified purpose stated in the notice. Fresh consent would be required for marketing communications.

---

**Q6. Under Section 8(7)(b), when must a Data Fiduciary cause its Data Processors to erase personal data?**

- A) When the Data Processor requests it
- B) When the Data Protection Board orders it
- C) When the Data Fiduciary erases the data — the Data Processor must also erase it
- D) After 3 years from the date of processing

✅ **Correct Answer: C**

**Explanation:** Section 8(7)(b) specifically requires that the Data Fiduciary must cause its Data Processor to erase any personal data that was made available to it for processing. The erasure obligation flows from the Data Fiduciary down to all Data Processors engaged by it.

---

**Q7. An individual approached a Data Fiduciary for a specified purpose on 1st January. She neither contacted the Fiduciary again nor exercised any rights. What triggers data erasure under DPDPA?**

- A) The individual submitting a written erasure request
- B) The expiry of the prescribed time period during which no contact or rights exercise occurred
- C) The Data Fiduciary's annual review of inactive accounts
- D) An order from the Data Protection Board

✅ **Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Under Section 8(8), the purpose is deemed no longer served when both conditions are absent for the prescribed time period — no approach for performance of purpose AND no exercise of rights. The Third Schedule of the Rules then specifies the exact time periods for different categories of Data Fiduciaries.

---

**Q8. Under Rule 8(2) of DPDP Rules 2025, before erasing personal data, the Data Fiduciary must inform the Data Principal how many hours in advance?**

- A) 24 hours
- B) 36 hours
- C) 48 hours
- D) 72 hours

✅ **Correct Answer: C**

**Explanation:** Rule 8(2) states that at least forty-eight hours before completion of the time period for erasure, the Data Fiduciary shall inform the Data Principal that such personal data shall be erased upon completion of such period, unless she logs into her account or otherwise contacts the Fiduciary.

---

**Q9. An employer collects biometric data of employees for attendance tracking. After an employee resigns, the employer continues using that employee's biometric data to test a new attendance system. Under DPDPA, this is:**

- A) Lawful as the employer owns the system
- B) Unlawful as the specified purpose (attendance tracking) no longer exists for that employee
- C) Lawful if the employee gave consent during employment
- D) Lawful as long as the data is anonymized

✅ **Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Once the employee resigns, the specified purpose of attendance tracking is no longer served for that individual. Continuing to use their biometric data for testing a new system goes beyond the specified purpose and violates the purpose limitation principle under Section 6(1) and Section 8(7).

---

**Q10. Which section of DPDPA deals with the erasure obligation of a Data Fiduciary when the specified purpose is no longer served?**

- A) Section 6(4)
- B) Section 7(a)
- C) Section 8(7)
- D) Section 9(2)

✅ **Correct Answer: C**

**Explanation:** Section 8(7) specifically deals with the data erasure obligation. It states that unless retention is necessary for compliance with any law, the Data Fiduciary must erase personal data upon withdrawal of consent or as soon as it is reasonable to assume that the specified purpose is no longer being served.

# PRINCIPLE 5 — DATA MINIMIZATION

## 📖 Explanation

**Key Sections: Section 6(1) + Second Schedule of DPDP Rules 2025**

Data Minimization is the principle that **only the minimum amount of personal data necessary to fulfill the specified purpose should be collected and processed**. No excess data should be sought.

### Where it Appears in DPDPA:

**Section 6(1)** — Consent must be:
> "limited to such personal data as is **necessary** for such specified purpose"

**Second Schedule (Rule 5 & Rule 16)** — For State processing and research:
> "Processing is **limited to such personal data** as is necessary for such uses or achieving such purposes"

### Key Implications:
- Collecting more data than needed makes the excess collection **unlawful**
- The **scope of consent** itself is limited — even if a person consents to excess data collection, that part of consent is invalid under Section 6(2)
- Applies equally to **private Data Fiduciaries** (through consent rules) and **State processing** (through Second Schedule)
- Directly linked to **purpose limitation** — data must be minimal AND purposeful

### Practical Application:
| Scenario | Data Minimization Status |
|---|---|
| Telemedicine app collecting health data | ✅ Necessary |
| Telemedicine app collecting phone contacts | ❌ Not necessary |
| Delivery app collecting delivery address | ✅ Necessary |
| Delivery app collecting browsing history | ❌ Not necessary |
| Bank collecting KYC data | ✅ Necessary |
| Bank collecting social media passwords | ❌ Not necessary |

# PRINCIPLE 6 — DATA ACCURACY

## 📖 Explanation

**Key Sections: Section 8(3) + Section 12 of DPDPA 2023**

Data Accuracy requires that **personal data must be accurate, complete, and consistent**, especially when it is used for decision-making or when it is to be shared with others.

### Two Dimensions of Data Accuracy:

**1. Obligation of Data Fiduciary (Section 8(3)):**
When personal data is likely to be:
- Used to **make a decision affecting the Data Principal**, OR
- **Disclosed to another Data Fiduciary**

...then the Data Fiduciary must ensure **completeness, accuracy and consistency** of the data.

**2. Right of Data Principal (Section 12):**
Data Principal has the right to request:
- **Correction** of inaccurate or misleading data
- **Completion** of incomplete data
- **Updating** of outdated data
- **Erasure** of personal data (subject to retention requirements)

### Process for Correction (Section 12(2)):
Upon receiving a correction request, the Data Fiduciary must:
- Correct inaccurate or misleading personal data
- Complete incomplete personal data
- Update the personal data

### Practical Importance:
| Situation | Accuracy Obligation |
|---|---|
| Bank sharing credit score with insurance company | Must ensure data is accurate |
| Government using census data to determine welfare eligibility | Must ensure data is complete |
| Employer sharing employee performance data with background checker | Must ensure accuracy and consistency |

# PRINCIPLE 7 — STORAGE LIMITATION

## 📖 Explanation

**Key Sections: Section 8(7), Section 8(8) + Rule 8 + Third Schedule of DPDP Rules 2025**

Storage Limitation requires that **personal data must not be retained longer than is necessary for the specified purpose** or as required by law.

### Two Storage Limitation Rules:

**Rule A — Purpose-Based Erasure (Section 8(7)):**
Data must be erased when:
- Data Principal **withdraws consent**, OR
- The **specified purpose is no longer being served**

**Rule B — Time-Based Deemed Expiry (Section 8(8) + Rule 8):**
Purpose is deemed no longer served when the Data Principal has not:
- Approached the Data Fiduciary for performance of the purpose, AND
- Exercised any rights in relation to such processing
...for the **prescribed time periods** in the Third Schedule

### Prescribed Retention Periods (Third Schedule — Rule 8):

| Category | Threshold | Retention Period |
|---|---|---|
| E-commerce entity | 2 crore+ registered users | 3 years from last contact |
| Online gaming intermediary | 50 lakh+ registered users | 3 years from last contact |
| Social media intermediary | 2 crore+ registered users | 3 years from last contact |

### Minimum Log Retention (Rule 8(3)):
- All Data Fiduciaries must retain **personal data, traffic data and processing logs for minimum 1 year** from the date of processing
- After 1 year → must erase unless law requires longer retention

### Pre-Erasure Notice (Rule 8(2)):
- Data Fiduciary must inform Data Principal **at least 48 hours before** erasure
- Unless she logs in or contacts the Fiduciary before expiry

# PRINCIPLE 8 — SECURITY & INTEGRITY

## 📖 Explanation

**Key Sections: Section 8(5), Section 8(6) + Rule 6 + Rule 7 of DPDP Rules 2025**

The Security & Integrity principle requires that **Data Fiduciaries must implement reasonable security safeguards to protect personal data from breaches, unauthorized access, destruction, and loss**.

### Security Safeguards Required (Section 8(5) + Rule 6):

Data Fiduciaries must implement, at minimum:

| Safeguard | Description |
|---|---|
| **Encryption/Obfuscation** | Protecting data through encryption, masking, or virtual tokens |
| **Access Controls** | Controlling who can access computer resources |
| **Logs & Monitoring** | Maintaining logs to detect unauthorized access |
| **Data Backups** | Ensuring continuity if data is lost or compromised |
| **Log Retention** | Retaining logs for minimum 1 year |
| **Processor Contracts** | Contracts with Data Processors must include security provisions |
| **Technical & Organisational Measures** | Overall systemic security governance |

### Breach Notification Requirements (Section 8(6) + Rule 7):

**To Data Principals (Rule 7(1)):**
- Notify **without delay**
- Through user account or registered communication means
- Include: breach description, consequences, mitigation measures, safety tips, contact information

**To the Data Protection Board (Rule 7(2)):**
- **Immediately** — initial brief description
- **Within 72 hours** — detailed information including:
  - Updated breach details
  - Broad facts, circumstances and reasons
  - Measures to mitigate risk
  - Findings about the person who caused the breach
  - Remedial measures to prevent recurrence
  - Report on intimations given to Data Principals

### Key Definitions:
- **Personal Data Breach** = any unauthorized processing, accidental disclosure, acquisition, sharing, use, alteration, destruction or loss of access that compromises confidentiality, integrity or availability
- **Computer Resource** = as defined in the IT Act 2000

# PRINCIPLE 9 — ACCOUNTABILITY

## 📖 Explanation

**Key Sections: Section 8(1), Section 8(2), Section 8(4) + Section 10 + Rule 13 of DPDP Rules 2025**

Accountability is the overarching principle that makes **Data Fiduciaries ultimately responsible for all aspects of data processing** — whether done by themselves or through Data Processors they engage.

### Core Accountability Obligations:

**1. Fiduciary Responsibility (Section 8(1)):**
- A Data Fiduciary is **responsible for complying with DPDPA** for any processing undertaken by it OR on its behalf by a Data Processor
- This applies **irrespective of any agreement to the contrary** or failure of the Data Principal to carry out duties

**2. Data Processor Engagement (Section 8(2)):**
- A Data Fiduciary may engage a Data Processor **only under a valid contract**
- The Fiduciary remains accountable even when work is outsourced

**3. Technical & Organisational Measures (Section 8(4)):**
- Must implement **appropriate technical and organisational measures** to ensure effective observance of the Act

**4. Additional Accountability for Significant Data Fiduciaries (Section 10 + Rule 13):**

| Obligation | Frequency |
|---|---|
| Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA) | Once every 12 months |
| Independent Data Audit | Once every 12 months |
| Appoint Data Protection Officer (DPO) | Ongoing |
| Appoint Independent Data Auditor | Ongoing |
| Verify algorithmic software for Data Principal rights risk | Ongoing |
| Data localization for specified data | As directed by Central Government |

### Who is a Significant Data Fiduciary? (Section 10(1))
Notified by Central Government based on assessment of:
- Volume and sensitivity of data processed
- Risk to rights of Data Principals
- Potential impact on sovereignty and integrity of India
- Risk to electoral democracy
- Security of the State
- Public order

## ❓ MCQs — Principle 9: Accountability

**Q1. Under Section 8(1) of DPDPA, a Data Fiduciary is responsible for DPDPA compliance:**

- A) Only for processing it directly undertakes
- B) Only if the Data Principal fulfills her duties under the Act
- C) For all processing undertaken by it or on its behalf by a Data Processor, irrespective of any agreement
- D) Only for processing done in India

✅ **Correct Answer: C**

**Explanation:** Section 8(1) explicitly states that a Data Fiduciary shall be responsible for complying with DPDPA in respect of any processing undertaken by it or on its behalf by a Data Processor — irrespective of any agreement to the contrary or failure of the Data Principal to carry out duties. Accountability cannot be contracted away.

---

**Q2. A Data Fiduciary outsources data processing to a Data Processor who causes a data breach. Who is accountable under DPDPA?**

- A) Only the Data Processor who caused the breach
- B) The Data Fiduciary and the Data Processor jointly
- C) The Data Fiduciary remains ultimately responsible even for actions of the Data Processor
- D) Accountability depends on the contractual terms between them

✅ **Correct Answer: C**

**Explanation:** Section 8(1) makes the Data Fiduciary responsible for all processing, including that done by Data Processors on its behalf, irrespective of any agreement. The Data Fiduciary cannot use outsourcing as a shield from accountability. While the Data Processor may also be liable, the Fiduciary's accountability remains.

**Q3. Under Section 8(2), a Data Fiduciary may engage a Data Processor:**

- A) Through a verbal authorization
- B) Only with prior approval of the Data Protection Board
- C) Only under a valid contract
- D) Only if the Data Processor is registered with the Board

✅ **Correct Answer: C**

**Explanation:** Section 8(2) specifically states that a Data Fiduciary may engage, appoint, use or otherwise involve a Data Processor to process personal data on its behalf only under a valid contract. This contractual requirement is a key accountability mechanism ensuring the Fiduciary maintains control and responsibility.

**Q4. Under Rule 13(1), how often must a Significant Data Fiduciary undertake a Data Protection Impact Assessment and audit?**

- A) Every 6 months
- B) Once every 12 months from the date of notification
- C) Every 2 years
- D) Only when directed by the Data Protection Board

✅ **Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Rule 13(1) states that a Significant Data Fiduciary shall, once in every period of twelve months from the date on which it is notified as such or included in the class of Data Fiduciaries notified as such, undertake a Data Protection Impact Assessment and an audit. This is an annual mandatory requirement.

**Q5. Under Section 10(2)(a), a Data Protection Officer (DPO) appointed by a Significant Data Fiduciary must:**

- A) Be a qualified lawyer with 10 years of experience
- B) Be based in India and be responsible to the Board of Directors
- C) Be approved by the Data Protection Board
- D) Be a government nominee with data governance expertise

✅ **Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Section 10(2)(a) specifies that the DPO must: (i) represent the Significant Data Fiduciary under the Act, (ii) be based in India, (iii) be an individual responsible to the Board of Directors or similar governing body, and (iv) be the point of contact for the grievance redressal mechanism. Being based in India and responsible to the Board of Directors are key accountability requirements.

**Q6. Under Rule 13(2), the Data Protection Impact Assessment report must be furnished to the Board by:**

- B) The DPO of the Significant Data Fiduciary
- C) The person carrying out the DPIA and audit
- D) The Central Government on behalf of the Significant Data Fiduciary

✅ **Correct Answer: C**

**Explanation:** Rule 13(2) states that a Significant Data Fiduciary shall cause the person carrying out the Data Protection Impact Assessment and audit to furnish to the Board a report containing significant observations. This ensures independence — the report comes directly from the assessor to the Board.

**Q7. Which of the following factors is NOT considered by the Central Government when notifying a Data Fiduciary as a Significant Data Fiduciary under Section 10(1)?**

- D) Risk to electoral democracy

Annual revenue and market capitalization are not listed as factors.

**Q8. Under Rule 13(3), a Significant Data Fiduciary must verify that its algorithmic software used for processing is:**

- D) Certified by an independent IT auditor annually

✅ **Correct Answer: B**

**Explanation:** Rule 13(3) requires that a Significant Data Fiduciary shall observe due diligence to verify that technical measures including algorithmic software adopted by it for hosting, display, uploading, modification, publishing, transmission, storage, updating or sharing of personal data processed by it are not likely to pose a risk to the rights of Data Principals.

**Q9. Under Section 8(4), the accountability principle requires that a Data Fiduciary implement:**

**Q10. Under Rule 13(4), a Significant Data Fiduciary may be required to ensure that specified personal data is processed subject to data localization restrictions. Who recommends which data must be localized?**
